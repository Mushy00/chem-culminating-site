Ammonia Production and Why Efficiency Matters

Article link: Chemical & Engineering News — “Industrial ammonia production emits more CO₂ than any other chemical-making reaction”

This article explains why ammonia production is extremely important (especially for fertilizers) and why it also has a major environmental cost. Ammonia is made mainly through the Haber-Bosch process, where nitrogen and hydrogen react to form ammonia in a reversible reaction. The purpose of the article is to show how large-scale chemistry connects to real-world needs (food supply) and to real-world problems (CO₂ emissions).

The article highlights that improving the process requires better efficiency and cleaner sources of hydrogen and energy. It concludes that chemistry innovation is necessary to keep producing ammonia for society while lowering environmental harm.

Connections to SCH4U Chemical Equilibrium (E3)

  • Reversible reaction: Haber process is an equilibrium system (both forward and reverse reactions occur).
  • Equilibrium position: Conditions can be adjusted to change the percent yield of NH3.
  • Le Châtelier’s Principle: Increasing pressure favors the side with fewer moles of gas (more NH3).
  • Temperature trade-off: Higher temperature increases rate but can lower equilibrium yield (typical equilibrium compromise).
  • Industrial decision-making: Industry balances yield, rate, cost, and safety—exactly what equilibrium problems model.

Moral / Ethical Issues Connection (E1)

This topic has a moral tension: ammonia supports global food supply, but the production contributes to climate change. Ethically, society must aim for solutions that protect both human life (food security) and the planet (lower emissions), rather than choosing one and ignoring the other.
