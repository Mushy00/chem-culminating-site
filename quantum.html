Quantum Sensors for Biomedical Applications

Article link: Nature Reviews Physics — “Quantum sensors for biomedical applications”

This review article explains how quantum physics enables extremely sensitive measurement tools used in medicine and biology. Quantum sensors can detect very small magnetic or electrical signals by using quantum properties of atoms and electrons. The purpose is to show how quantum effects—normally studied at atomic scales—can be used to improve imaging and measurement in real biomedical settings.

The article highlights examples such as advanced magnetic sensing for brain activity and precise detection methods connected to spectroscopy. It concludes that quantum technology is moving toward practical medical tools that could improve diagnosis and research, as engineering makes these systems more reliable and accessible.

Connections to SCH4U Quantum Model of the Atom (D3)

  • Electron behavior is probabilistic: Quantum mechanics describes electrons using orbitals and probability, not fixed paths.
  • Energy levels: Quantum systems have discrete energy states; transitions can be measured and used for sensing.
  • Wave–particle duality: Matter behaves like waves and particles, which is foundational to how quantum devices operate.
  • Atomic-scale structure matters: Understanding atomic and molecular structure supports spectroscopy and high-precision measurement.

Moral / Ethical Issues Connection (D1)

Medical technology raises ethical responsibilities: privacy, fairness in access, and safety testing. If quantum sensing improves diagnosis, it should not only benefit a small group. Ethical science means protecting patient dignity, using data responsibly, and making technology accessible when possible.
