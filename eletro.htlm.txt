    <h1>Fuel Cells: Clean Electricity from Redox Reactions</h1>
    <p class="muted">
      Article link:
      <a href="https://www.energy.gov/eere/fuelcells/fuel-cells" target="_blank" rel="noopener">
        U.S. Department of Energy — “Fuel Cells”
      </a>
    </p>

    <h2>Abstract (1–2 paragraphs)</h2>
    <p>
      This page explains how fuel cells generate electricity by converting chemical energy directly into electrical energy through electrochemical reactions.
      In many fuel cells, hydrogen is oxidized and oxygen is reduced, producing water and electrical energy. The purpose is to show how fuel cells can provide
      efficient, low-emission power for transportation and stationary energy systems.
    </p>
    <p>
      The article emphasizes that fuel cells can be cleaner than combustion because they avoid burning fuel and can be highly efficient.
      It concludes that fuel cells are a key technology for reducing emissions, especially when hydrogen is produced using low-carbon methods.
    </p>

    <h2>Connections to SCH4U Electrochemistry (F3)</h2>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Oxidation and reduction:</strong> Redox reactions drive electron flow through an external circuit.</li>
      <li><strong>Anode/cathode:</strong> Oxidation occurs at the anode, reduction occurs at the cathode (electrochemical cell rules).</li>
      <li><strong>Cell potential (E°):</strong> The voltage depends on the difference in reduction potentials of the half-reactions.</li>
      <li><strong>Electrolyte and ion movement:</strong> Ions move internally to maintain charge balance while electrons move through the wire.</li>
    </ul>

    <h2>Moral / Ethical Issues Connection (F1)</h2>
    <p>
      Clean energy technology raises ethical questions about resource use, safety, and fairness.
      For example, how hydrogen is produced matters: if it is made using fossil fuels with no carbon control,
      the environmental benefits shrink. Ethical decision-making means evaluating the full life cycle of the technology.
    </p>

    <h2>Ontario Catholic Graduate Expectation: A Responsible Citizen</h2>
    <p>
      Fuel cell technology supports responsible citizenship because it aims to reduce pollution that harms human health and ecosystems.
      Using electrochemistry to create cleaner power shows stewardship of creation and a commitment to the common good.
    </p>
